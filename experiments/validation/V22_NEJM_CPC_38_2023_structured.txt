## Phase 1 — Initial Assessment

### A. Problem Representation
A 68-year-old woman with a history of recurrent venous thrombosis (on apixaban after long-term warfarin), pulmonary hypertension, and autoimmune comorbidity (vitiligo, hypothyroidism) developed subacute progressive generalized choreiform/athetoid movements followed by worsening encephalopathy, with MRI showing recurrent, multifocal acute-to-subacute ischemic infarcts in multiple vascular territories (cortical and subcortical, bilateral, plus cerebellum) despite therapeutic anticoagulation, accompanied by anemia and leukopenia and a mildly prolonged aPTT.

### B. Initial Differential Diagnosis (Top 5, ranked)

1) **Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS), likely autoimmune (primary APS or SLE-associated), with arterial thromboembolism and APS-associated chorea**  
   **Leading diagnosis probability: 60%**  
   **Reasoning:** Prior DVTs + new recurrent multifocal ischemic strokes in multiple territories while on a DOAC (apixaban)—a known failure scenario in high-risk APS; mildly prolonged aPTT compatible with lupus anticoagulant effect; autoimmune background (vitiligo/thyroid disease) and cytopenias (anemia, leukopenia) fit an autoimmune systemic process; chorea is a recognized neurologic manifestation of APS/SLE and can precede or accompany ischemic events.

2) **Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE; marantic endocarditis) due to occult malignancy or systemic autoimmune disease**  
   Multifocal “embolic shower” infarcts across vascular territories; anemia could reflect malignancy/chronic disease. TTE can miss small vegetations (TEE often needed).

3) **Infective endocarditis with cerebral emboli**  
   Multi-territory infarcts are compatible; however afebrile, leukopenic rather than leukocytotic, and no supportive systemic infectious features provided—still a “can’t miss.”

4) **Primary CNS vasculitis / systemic inflammatory vasculitis with CNS involvement**  
   Can cause multifocal ischemia and encephalopathy; however MRA shows no stenosis/irregularity (not sensitive), and the strong thrombotic history pushes higher toward APS/NBTE.

5) **Occult atrial fibrillation or other cardioembolic source not captured on telemetry (e.g., atrial cardiopathy, left atrial appendage thrombus, valvular disease)**  
   Pattern is embolic; telemetry/TTE were unrevealing but do not exclude paroxysmal AF or small structural lesions.

## Phase 2 — Structured Second Look

### A. Problem Representation Check
- Key discriminating features that must be explicit: **(1) recurrent venous thrombosis**, **(2) recurrent multifocal ischemic infarcts in multiple territories despite apixaban**, **(3) progressive chorea + encephalopathy**, **(4) autoimmune diathesis/cytopenias**, **(5) mildly prolonged aPTT**.  
- The leading diagnosis (APS) matches the abstracted illness script: hypercoagulable autoimmune state → venous and arterial thrombosis; DOAC failure in high-risk APS; neurologic manifestations including stroke and chorea.

### B. Stress Test the Leading Diagnosis (APS)
- **Strongest argument against APS:** The MRI lesions are described mainly as cortical and subcortical infarcts (embolus-like distribution) rather than a classic basal ganglia lacunar pattern one might expect to “explain” chorea. Also, APS is not confirmed serologically here (no antibody data yet), and aPTT interpretation is confounded by anticoagulation.
- **Findings not fully explained by APS:** The **severity of anemia (Hgb 7.9)** is not automatically explained by APS itself and raises concern for bleeding, hemolysis, marrow disease, or malignancy—each of which could shift toward NBTE/malignancy-associated thrombosis.

### C. Consider Alternatives
- **Single best alternative:** **NBTE from occult malignancy (or autoimmune disease)**  
  **Supporting findings over APS:** Multi-territory cortical infarcts strongly suggest an embolic shower; severe anemia could be malignancy-related; NBTE can occur with systemic autoimmune disease and may not be seen on TTE (TEE required).
- **“Can’t miss” diagnosis:** **Infective endocarditis** (requires blood cultures/TEE promptly because missing it changes management and is high morbidity). Also **TTP** is a can’t-miss in neuro + anemia, but normal platelets make it less likely.

### D. Defend or Update
- **Findings that most strongly support APS:** Prior recurrent DVTs; arterial events (strokes) while on a DOAC; autoimmune background; mild aPTT prolongation; chorea as a known APS/SLE manifestation.
- **APS vs NBTE overall fit:**  
  - APS better explains the *combined* venous + arterial thrombotic history and DOAC failure pattern, and can coexist with pulmonary hypertension.  
  - NBTE better explains an embolic shower pattern and could relate to anemia/malignancy, but does not inherently explain the long-standing DVT history unless malignancy is chronic/occult or there is a parallel thrombophilia.
- **Update decision:** Keep APS as leading, but with a **high-priority competing diagnosis of NBTE**; the unexplained anemia is clinically significant and mandates evaluation for malignancy/bleeding/hemolysis and for valvular vegetations.
