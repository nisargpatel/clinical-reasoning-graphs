## Phase 1: INITIAL ASSESSMENT

### A. PROBLEM REPRESENTATION

A 30-year-old woman with multiple autoimmune conditions presents with 3 years of episodic, progressive leg stiffness and rigidity causing gait abnormality, exaggerated startle response, nocturnal myoclonus, temporary improvement with benzodiazepines, hyperreflexia with ankle clonus, and lumbar hyperlordosis, without evidence of structural spinal pathology.

### B. Initial Differential Diagnosis

1. **Stiff-person syndrome (SPS)** - 75%
2. Progressive encephalomyelitis with rigidity and myoclonus (PERM) - 10%
3. Hereditary spastic paraplegia - 8%
4. Functional neurological disorder - 5%
5. Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD) - 2%

**Leading Diagnosis: Stiff-person syndrome (75%)**

**Reasoning:**
The constellation of findings strongly suggests stiff-person syndrome:
- **Progressive axial and limb rigidity/stiffness**: Classic distribution affecting paraspinal and leg muscles
- **Episodic spasms**: The "locking up" episodes and stiffening leading to falls
- **Exaggerated startle response**: Pathognomonic feature
- **Hyperreflexia with clonus**: Upper motor neuron signs from continuous muscle activity
- **Lumbar hyperlordosis**: Classic postural abnormality from continuous paraspinal muscle contraction
- **Dramatic response to benzodiazepines**: Lorazepam temporarily normalized her gait (GABAergic agents are therapeutic in SPS)
- **Nocturnal myoclonus**: Sleep-related spasms typical of SPS
- **Strong autoimmune background**: Vitiligo, Graves' disease, immune thrombocytopenia, alopecia areata, family history of autoimmune disease—SPS is frequently associated with other autoimmune conditions, particularly type 1 diabetes and thyroid disease
- **Normal inflammatory markers and imaging**: Excludes most structural/inflammatory causes

The young age and autoimmune predisposition fit classic SPS demographics.

### A. PROBLEM REPRESENTATION CHECK

My problem representation appropriately led to the SPS illness script. I did not skip steps—the pattern of rigidity + startle + benzodiazepine response + autoimmunity + hyperlordosis directly activates the SPS script.

### B. STRESS TEST LEADING DIAGNOSIS

**Strongest argument AGAINST stiff-person syndrome:**
- **Relatively long duration without more severe disability**: At 3 years, classic SPS often progresses to more severe rigidity and disability
- **Predominant leg involvement initially**: While SPS can affect legs, the axial (truncal) musculature is typically most prominently involved early
- **Initial waxing/waning pattern**: SPS is usually progressive rather than fluctuating, though this can occur

**Findings NOT explained by SPS:**
- The **early fluctuating course** with complete normalization is somewhat atypical
- **Guarded passive flexion improving with distraction** (rheumatology exam) could suggest a functional overlay, though pain/apprehension from spasms could explain this
- The **motion artifact on multiple MRI studies** is interesting—could this represent inability to remain still due to spasms, or anxiety/functional component?

### C. CONSIDER ALTERNATIVES

**Best alternative diagnosis: Functional Neurological Disorder (FND)**

**Specific findings supporting FND:**
- **Guarded passive flexion that improved with distraction**: Classic "give-way" weakness/guarding pattern
- **Disproportionate fear of falling** leading to exercise avoidance
- **Anxiety** noted on examination
- **Motion artifact on multiple MRIs**: Could suggest difficulty cooperating/remaining still
- **Wide-based gait with reduced knee flexion**: Could represent functional gait pattern
- **Young woman**: Demographic commonly affected by FND
- Initial **waxing and waning** course with complete periods of normality

However, against FND:
- **Objective findings**: Hyperreflexia, ankle clonus, exaggerated startle are difficult to feign
- **Dramatic benzodiazepine response**: This pharmacologic specificity is more consistent with organic pathology
- **Lumbar hyperlordosis**: Objective postural change
- **Progressive course**: FND can be progressive but the consistent worsening is more typical of organic disease

**Can't miss diagnosis: Progressive Encephalomyelitis with Rigidity and Myoclonus (PERM)**
- PERM is a more severe variant on the SPS spectrum
- Features: Rigidity, myoclonus, brainstem involvement, autonomic dysfunction
- This patient has rigidity, nocturnal myoclonus, and exaggerated startle (brainstem hyperexcitability)
- However, lacks severe brainstem signs, autonomic dysfunction, and has a more indolent course than typical PERM

### D. DEFEND OR UPDATE

**Findings MOST STRONGLY SUPPORTING SPS:**
1. **Exaggerated startle response**: This objective finding is pathognomonic and cannot be easily feigned
2. **Dramatic response to lorazepam**: "Normal gait for several hours" after benzodiazepine is highly specific for GABAergic deficiency states like SPS
3. **Continuous muscle activity** leading to lumbar hyperlordosis: Objective structural consequence
4. **Autoimmune clustering**: Multiple autoimmune conditions in patient and family strongly supports autoimmune SPS
5. **Ankle clonus and hyperreflexia**: Objective upper motor neuron signs from muscle overactivity

**Comparison with FND:**
- While some features could suggest functional overlay (guarded movements, anxiety, fear of falling), the **objective neurological signs** (startle response, clonus, hyperreflexia, hyperlordosis) and **specific pharmacologic response** to benzodiazepines strongly favor organic pathology
- The **autoimmune context** provides a clear mechanistic explanation for SPS but not for FND
- **Base rates**: In a young woman with anxiety and pain behaviors, FND is common, but the specific constellation of signs points away from purely functional etiology

**Is unexplained evidence significant?**
- The early fluctuating course and some behavioral features are not perfectly explained by SPS
- However, these could represent:
  - Early/mild disease before full development
  - Coexisting anxiety (reactive to chronic symptoms)
  - Normal variation in SPS presentation
- This is **expected noise** rather than evidence requiring diagnostic revision

The preponderance of objective findings, pharmacologic response specificity, and autoimmune context outweigh the atypical features.

The patient's **extensive autoimmune history** provides strong mechanistic support for autoimmune SPS.
