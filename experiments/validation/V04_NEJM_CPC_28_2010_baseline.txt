## 2) Leading Diagnosis with Reasoning

### Leading diagnosis: **Postpartum spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD)**

This presentation is most consistent with **acute coronary syndrome in a young postpartum woman without traditional atherosclerotic risk factors**, with **dynamic anterior ST-segment elevation** and classic ischemic symptoms (substernal pain radiating to the jaw). **SCAD is a leading cause of MI in pregnancy and the early postpartum period**, often involving the **LAD**, producing **anterolateral ST elevation** and sometimes **waxing/waning ischemia** as the intramural hematoma/dissection flap intermittently compromises coronary flow.

Key points supporting SCAD over atherosclerotic STEMI:
- **Timing and demographic:** 3 weeks postpartum, age 32, no smoking/drugs, no known dyslipidemia/diabetes.
- **ECG territory:** Marked transient **ST elevation V1–V6** with reciprocal inferior depression → strongly suggests **acute LAD-territory transmural ischemia**, which in this demographic is classically SCAD until proven otherwise.
- **Episodic pain with transient ST changes:** While vasospasm is a strong alternative, **SCAD can also present with intermittent ischemia** (dynamic obstruction from an evolving intramural hematoma or changing true-lumen compression).
- **Troponin initially negative does not exclude MI/SCAD:** She presented early; biomarkers can lag and may rise later.

### Step 2 — Explain why SCAD is #1
**Findings that support SCAD**
- **Postpartum state is a major SCAD risk window** (hormonal/arterial wall changes, hemodynamic stress).
- **Young woman, minimal traditional CAD risk factors** → makes plaque rupture less likely.
- **LAD-territory ischemia** is common in SCAD.
- **Dynamic ischemia** can occur in SCAD (propagation, intermittent true lumen compression, superimposed spasm).

**Findings that might argue against SCAD**
- **Very brief, fully reversible episodes** can be more typical of pure vasospasm.
- No biomarker elevation yet (but early).

**Why it still ranks above vasospasm:** postpartum status strongly shifts pre-test probability toward SCAD among life-threatening coronary diagnoses, and the initial ECG changes were large and strongly territorial (suggesting a substantial jeopardized myocardium), which SCAD commonly produces.

### Step 4 — Why postpartum thrombosis/embolism is #3
**Supporting**
- Postpartum is **hypercoagulable**, and acute coronary thrombosis (or coronary embolus) can occur.
- Placental findings (**small placenta, increased perivillous fibrin**) plus prior hypertensive pregnancy raise the possibility of an underlying **prothrombotic/vasculopathic disorder** (e.g., antiphospholipid syndrome), which could predispose to arterial thrombosis.

**Against**
- A thrombotic LAD occlusion usually causes **persistent** ischemia/ECG changes and escalating troponin rather than repeated brief episodes with full normalization (unless there is spontaneous lysis/re-occlusion, which is less typical).

### Step 5 — Why PE is #4

- Some shortness of breath and mild oxygen desaturation (95% on room air).

**Against**
- The ECG is not PE-typical; instead it shows a **territorial anterior STEMI pattern** with reciprocal changes.
- No pleuritic pain, no leg symptoms, normal CXR, and pain radiating to jaw is far more coronary than PE.

PE still must be considered clinically in postpartum patients, but it is not the best explanation for these ECG findings.

---

### Step 6 — Why Takotsubo/peripartum myocardial injury is #5
**Supporting**
- Occurs in women under acute stress; can present with chest pain and ST elevation.

**Against**
- Typically does **not** produce rapid on/off ST-elevation episodes tightly coupled to brief pain spells.
- More often associated with a sustained syndrome and echo findings (apical ballooning) rather than transient ischemic ECG normalization.
