This is much less likely than meningococcal disease, but worth considering because *Neisseria gonorrhoeae* can occasionally cause meningococcemia-like illness.

- Petechial/pustular rash can occur in DGI

- No joint symptoms or tenosynovitis

Thus possible but clearly lower probability.

### 3. Rocky Mountain spotted fever or other rickettsial infection — **Third**

- meningitic symptoms,
- **nonblanching petechial/purpuric rash**,
- thrombocytopenia,
- rapid progression,
- neutrophilic systemic and CSF inflammatory response.

That is the signature pattern of **invasive meningococcal infection**.

Even if one hesitated because the CSF glucose is normal and Gram stain is negative, the skin findings make the diagnosis far more likely. Meningococcemia can predominate, with relatively modest meningeal findings early. In fact, meningococcal disease can present as a bloodstream infection with rash and only subtle early CSF abnormalities.

The childhood episode of bacterial meningitis is a particularly valuable clue because recurrent invasive meningococcal disease should prompt suspicion for **terminal complement component deficiency (C5-C9)** or properdin deficiency. That does not change the acute diagnosis, but it strengthens the likelihood that this infection is due to *Neisseria meningitidis*.
