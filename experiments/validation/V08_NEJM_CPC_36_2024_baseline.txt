## Leading diagnosis with reasoning

This presentation is most suggestive of **acute intermittent porphyria (AIP)**, an acute hepatic porphyria causing episodic neurovisceral attacks.

Key features that point in this direction:

- **Recurrent severe abdominal pain with normal imaging and unrevealing routine labs.** AIP classically causes intense, poorly localized abdominal pain with nausea and vomiting but often no corresponding structural abnormality on ultrasound or CT.
- **Associated autonomic and neurologic-type symptoms:** fatigue, dizziness, generalized weakness, and constipation. Constipation is especially characteristic in porphyric attacks because of autonomic dysfunction affecting the gut.
- **Teenage girl with a potential hormonal trigger.** AIP often emerges after puberty and is more common in females; attacks can be precipitated by hormones, including progesterone-containing medications or cyclic hormonal changes. She is taking oral contraceptives, which is a notable clue.
- **Pain episodes are abrupt, severe, and recurrent over weeks**, with vomiting that sometimes follows the pain. This episodic neurovisceral pattern is highly compatible with porphyria.

- **Common alternatives fit less well.** Peptic disease could cause epigastric pain and nausea, but repeated abrupt brief attacks, marked constipation, weakness, and entirely normal imaging with no meal relation are less typical. Abdominal migraine is possible, but constipation and the overall neurovisceral/autonomic pattern plus the age/sex/hormonal context make AIP stronger.

AIP does not require hyponatremia or obvious neuropathy to be present early; those are supportive but not necessary. The absence of psychiatric decompensation or dark urine also does not exclude it.
