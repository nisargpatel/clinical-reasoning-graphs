### 1) Lyme disease with Lyme carditis ± neuroborreliosis — **Rank #1**
**Supporting findings**
- **AV conduction disease** (PR prolongation + Mobitz I) in a young adult is a classic red flag for Lyme carditis (conduction disease can evolve rapidly and fluctuate).
- **Systemic symptoms**: fatigue, myalgias, chills, headache—common in disseminated Lyme.
- **Exposure**: forested town + hiking/camping strongly increases pretest probability.
- **Possible neuro involvement**: preceding **neck/upper back pain radiating down the arm** can be radicular pain from Lyme neuroborreliosis; this symptom is otherwise a bit atypical as a “simple strain,” especially given subsequent systemic/cardiac evolution.
- **Chest symptoms** (tightness/pleuritic/positional) can occur with associated myopericardial inflammation in Lyme.

**Arguments against / limitations**
- No reported erythema migrans, tick bite, or fever (but these are absent in a substantial fraction).
- PR interval (240 ms) is prolonged but **not** >300 ms; however, Lyme carditis can present across a spectrum and can worsen quickly.

**Why #1**
- Among reversible causes of new AV block in a young, exposed patient, Lyme is the most characteristic and actionable diagnosis with potentially serious progression (high-grade AV block).

### 2) Post–COVID myopericarditis — **Rank #2**
**Supporting findings**
- Timing: symptoms occurred within weeks of SARS‑CoV‑2 reinfection.
- **Pleuritic/positional chest pain** suggests pericarditis physiology.
- **Conduction abnormalities** can accompany myocarditis.
- **BNP elevation** (604 pg/mL) suggests some degree of myocardial stress even with normal troponin.

**Arguments against**
- **Troponin repeatedly normal** and **CRP/ESR normal**, which lowers (but does not eliminate) the likelihood of active myopericarditis.
- The patient recently received a **methylprednisolone taper**, which could blunt inflammatory markers and symptoms—making interpretation harder, but also making a robust inflammatory syndrome less evident.

**Why #2**
- COVID-related cardiac inflammation is plausible, but the combination of **AV block pattern + strong environmental exposure** favors Lyme over COVID as the unifying explanation.

---

### 3) Other viral myocarditis with conduction involvement — **Rank #3**
**Supporting findings**
- Myocarditis can produce chest discomfort, dyspnea, palpitations, BNP rise, and conduction abnormalities.
- Systemic viral symptoms can be subtle.

**Arguments against**
- Lack of typical viral prodrome (fever, URI symptoms) and persistently normal troponin/markers reduce probability.
- Does not explain the exposure-driven risk signal as well as Lyme/Q fever.

**Why #3**
- Remains a general, important alternative diagnosis when conduction disease and cardiopulmonary symptoms arise, but less specifically supported here than Lyme or post-COVID inflammation.

---

### 4) Q fever (Coxiella burnetii) — **Rank #4**
**Supporting findings**
- **Sheep exposure** is a notable epidemiologic risk.
- Q fever commonly causes **hepatitis (transaminitis)** and systemic symptoms; it can also cause **myocarditis/pericarditis** and (more classically) endocarditis (usually subacute/chronic).

**Arguments against**
- No prominent high fever, severe headache pattern typical of acute Q fever, or pneumonia.
- AV block is not a hallmark feature.

**Why #4**
- Epidemiology + transaminitis keeps it on the list, but the conduction phenotype fits Lyme better.

---

### 5) Cardiac sarcoidosis / other inflammatory-infiltrative myocarditis — **Rank #5**
**Supporting findings**
- Can cause AV block and ventricular arrhythmias in young/middle-aged adults.

**Arguments against**
- Presentation is relatively acute/subacute after an infectious-like syndrome.
- CT angiogram reportedly showed no mediastinal/hilar lymphadenopathy (not definitive, but lowers likelihood).
- No extracardiac sarcoid features described.

**Why #5**
- Important “can’t miss” consideration with AV block, but overall clinical context is more infectious/postinfectious than infiltrative.
