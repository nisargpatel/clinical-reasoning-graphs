## Phase 1

**Initial Assessment**

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1.  **Coronary Artery Vasospasm (Prinzmetal's/Variant Angina):** The clinical hallmark of this case is recurrent substernal chest pain associated with marked, transient ST-segment elevation (up to 4 mm anteriorly) that resolves completely between episodes. This dynamic supply-side ischemia is the textbook presentation of variant angina. The patient's history of preeclampsia and placental insufficiency points toward widespread endothelial dysfunction, a key mechanism in vasospastic angina.
2.  **Spontaneous Coronary Artery Dissection (SCAD):** This is the single most common cause of pregnancy-associated myocardial infarction (PAMI) and a "can't miss" diagnosis in a 3-week postpartum woman. While usually associated with persistent obstruction and MI, SCAD can present with stuttering symptoms and dynamic ECG changes if the dissection flap creates a mobile obstruction or triggers intense secondary vasospasm.
3.  **Coronary Embolism (Paradoxical or Thrombotic):** The postpartum state is hypercoagulable (elevated D-dimer supports this), increasing the risk of DVT and paradoxical embolism via a patent foramen ovale (PFO). However, the repetitive, uniform nature of the episodes ("on-off" pattern) is less characteristic of embolic showering, which typically causes a single acute event.
4.  **Aortic Dissection:** The severe pain and elevated blood pressure raise concern for Type A dissection involving the coronary ostia. However, the complete resolution of pain with nitrates/morphine and the absence of pulse deficits or mediastinal widening on CXR make this less likely.
5.  **Acute Pulmonary Embolism:** A leading cause of postpartum chest pain. The elevated D-dimer and "distended jugular veins" support this. However, the ECG findings of focal, profound anterolateral ST-elevation resolving within minutes are inconsistent with the right ventricular strain patterns seen in massive PE.

**Leading Diagnosis:** **Coronary Artery Vasospasm (Variant Angina)**
**Estimated Probability:** 60%
**Reasoning:** The phenomenology of the episode—transient transmural ischemia (STE) leading to immediate complete resolution—is the defining feature of coronary vasospasm. In a young woman with a history of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (a marker for endothelial dysfunction), this fits biologically and clinically.

## Phase 2

**Adversarial Self-Critique**

**Strongest Argument AGAINST Leading Diagnosis (Vasospasm):**
The strongest counter-argument is the pre-test probability of **SCAD** in the postpartum period. A healthy 32-year-old woman presenting with STEMI-like changes in the puerperium has SCAD until proven otherwise. Furthermore, pure vasospasm does not typically elevate the **D-dimer** to 1,166 ng/ml. While pregnancy elevates D-dimer, such a high level at 3 weeks postpartum suggests active fibrinolysis of a thrombus or hematoma, which is present in SCAD (intramural hematoma) but absent in pure vasospasm.

**Findings NOT Explained by Leading Diagnosis:**
1.  **Elevated D-Dimer:** As noted, this suggests clot burden or hematoma turnover.
2.  **Placental Pathology:** The small placenta (<5th percentile) and increased perivillous fibrin suggest **Maternal Vascular Malperfusion**. This histology is often a proxy for systemic arteriopathies like Fibromuscular Dysplasia (FMD). FMD is the strongest risk factor for SCAD. Pure vasospasm is related to endothelial dysfunction but lacks the structural arterial fragility implied by the placental findings.

**Alternative Diagnosis Explaining Findings Better:**
**Spontaneous Coronary Artery Dissection (SCAD):**
SCAD unifies the "Postpartum" timing, the "Placental/Vascular" pathology (suggesting FMD), and the "Elevated D-Dimer" (intramural hematoma). The transient nature of the ECG changes, which drove my initial diagnosis of spasm, can be explained in SCAD by:
*   An intimal flap acting as a "ball-valve," causing intermittent total occlusion.
*   The dissection triggering severe, superimposed vasospasm.
In this model, the "vasospasm" is the *symptom*, but SCAD is the *disease*. Treating for simple spasm (e.g., just nitrates) without imaging could miss the underlying fragile anatomy.

**Can't Miss Diagnosis:**
**SCAD** is the can't miss. Misdiagnosis (e.g., treating as atherosclerotic plaque with aggressive antiplatelets/anticoagulants, or attempting catheterization without extreme care) can propagate the dissection and cause catastrophic vessel closure.
