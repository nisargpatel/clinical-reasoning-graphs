## Key Clinical Features

**Temporal Progression:**
- Week 0: Acute stroke symptoms (dysarthria, dizziness) with small left frontal infarct
- Weeks 1-6: Progressive involuntary movements (chorea) involving legs → arms → tongue
- Week 7-9: Multiple falls, widespread chorea

**Movement Disorder Characteristics:**
- Nonrhythmic, dancelike, writhing movements (classic chorea)
- Involves face, tongue, trunk, and all limbs
- Partially suppressible
- Normal EEG (not seizures)

**Imaging Pattern:**
- Multiple scattered small infarcts in different vascular territories
- Both cortical and subcortical involvement
- Bilateral cerebral hemispheres and cerebellum
- No large vessel occlusion or stenosis

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Anemia (Hgb 7.9, Hct 26%) - significant
- Leukopenia with lymphopenia
- Mildly elevated INR and aPTT
- Elevated ESR

**Relevant History:**
- Two prior DVTs
- Vitiligo (autoimmune condition)
- Multiple vascular risk factors

## Diagnostic Reasoning

The combination of:
1. **Multifocal strokes in different vascular territories** without large vessel disease
2. **Progressive chorea** 
3. **Cytopenias** (anemia, leukopenia, lymphopenia)
4. **Prolonged aPTT** 
5. **History of recurrent DVTs**
6. **Vitiligo** (associated autoimmune condition)

This constellation strongly suggests **Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)**. The mechanism explains:
- Recurrent arterial (strokes) and venous (DVTs) thromboses
- Chorea is a well-recognized neurological manifestation of APS
- Cytopenias can occur in APS
- Prolonged aPTT paradoxically associated with thrombosis (lupus anticoagulant)
- Association with other autoimmune conditions (vitiligo)

The switch from warfarin to apixaban 10 months ago may be significant, as DOACs may be less effective than warfarin in APS, particularly in high-risk patients.

## Differential Diagnosis

1. **Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)** - Primary or secondary to SLE
2. **Systemic Lupus Erythematosus with CNS involvement** - Cytopenias, autoimmune features, could cause strokes and chorea
3. **Sneddon Syndrome** - Livedo reticularis with strokes (often APS-related)
4. **Infective Endocarditis** - Embolic strokes, anemia; but no fever, heart findings
5. **Cerebral Vasculitis** - Multifocal strokes, but typically different imaging pattern

## Arguments For/Against Leading Diagnoses

**For APS:**
- Recurrent DVTs + multiple arterial strokes = dual thrombotic tendency
- Chorea is characteristic of APS
- Prolonged aPTT with thrombosis (lupus anticoagulant)
- Cytopenias
- Worsening after switching from warfarin to apixaban

**Against other diagnoses:**
- No fever/murmur against endocarditis
- No rash/arthritis/serositis against primary SLE (though could be secondary APS)
- Vasculitis less likely given thrombotic pattern and prolonged aPTT

**Leading Diagnosis:** Antiphospholipid Syndrome (Catastrophic APS or APS with CNS involvement)
