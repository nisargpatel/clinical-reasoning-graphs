## Phase 1 — Initial Assessment

### A. Problem Representation (1 sentence)
A 34-year-old former special-operations combat medic with multiple prior blast/impact exposures and a prolonged loss-of-consciousness concussion presents with **chronic post-traumatic headaches** plus **progressive cognitive/mood/behavioral changes and trauma-related intrusive symptoms (flashbacks, hypervigilance, panic)** and more recent **prominent nausea/vomiting with weight loss**, in the setting of **heavy alcohol use and daily cannabis use**, with a normal general/neurologic exam and largely unremarkable basic labs.

### B. Initial Differential Diagnosis (Top 5, ranked)
1. **Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) with panic attacks and depressive symptoms**, with prominent somatic/autonomic symptoms (vomiting, chest/arm paresthesias)  
2. **Persistent postconcussive symptoms / mild neurocognitive disorder due to traumatic brain injury (TBI)** (headaches, irritability, sleep disturbance, cognitive complaints)  
3. **Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (CHS)** (daily cannabis + morning emesis, chronic nausea, weight loss)  
4. **Alcohol use disorder with alcohol-related gastritis/dyspepsia and anxiety amplification** (daily large whiskey intake; GI symptoms and mood lability)  
5. **Primary neurologic/structural disorder mimicking psychiatric illness** (e.g., intracranial mass/raised ICP, seizures, inflammatory CNS disease) — lower probability but important to exclude given vomiting + cognitive change

**Leading diagnosis:** **PTSD with panic attacks (with comorbid TBI-related postconcussive symptoms and substance use contributing)**  
**Estimated probability:** **60%**

**Reasoning (why this is most likely):**
- The symptom cluster strongly matches PTSD: **intrusive recollections/flashbacks**, **hypervigilance/easy startle**, **avoidance** (won’t go to malls; avoids driving), **negative mood/cognition** (anhedonia, detachment, guilt/burden), **irritability**, and **sleep disturbance**.
- Episodes of **chest pain and left arm tingling** temporally linked to traumatic memories are classic for **panic/autonomic surges** rather than focal neurologic disease.
- His cognitive/behavioral changes are plausibly multifactorial (PTSD/depression + sleep disruption + alcohol/cannabis + prior TBI), and his headaches fit common post-TBI patterns (migraine phenotype plus cervicogenic/occipital neuralgia).
- The **new GI syndrome** could be driven by anxiety/trauma triggers, but daily cannabis use makes CHS a strong comorbidity.

### B. Stress Test of Leading Diagnosis (PTSD with panic attacks)
**Strongest argument against PTSD as the primary/unifying diagnosis:**
- **Persistent morning emesis and significant unintentional weight loss** can indicate **organic GI disease** or **intracranial pathology**, and PTSD alone should not be accepted as an explanation without excluding these.

**Findings not fully explained by PTSD alone:**
- Two distinct, persistent headache syndromes (one migraine-like, one occipital “electric” pain with neck movement) suggest **neuropathic/cervicogenic mechanisms**.
- Cognitive changes (topographic disorientation, notable forgetfulness) could be **TBI-related neurocognitive disorder** beyond what PTSD/depression typically produces.
- Chronic daily cannabis use raises CHS as a separate driver of vomiting.

### C. Consider Alternatives
**Single best alternative diagnosis:** **Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome**
- Supports: **daily marijuana use**, **recurrent morning vomiting**, chronic nausea/abdominal pain, weight loss, extensive acid-suppression regimen with persistent symptoms.
- Against: the case description does not mention hallmark behavior (compulsive hot showers/baths), and vomiting is sometimes described as occurring with flashbacks (suggesting a psychophysiologic trigger).

**“Can’t miss” diagnosis to consider:**
- **Intracranial mass/raised intracranial pressure** (morning vomiting + headaches + cognitive change). Even with a normal exam, this warrants exclusion with neuroimaging if not already done.
- Also, given weight loss and persistent upper abdominal pain: **peptic ulcer disease/complicated GERD, biliary disease, malignancy** (less likely at 34, but still requires appropriate evaluation).

### D. Defend or Update
**Findings that most strongly support PTSD/panic as primary:**
- Clear trauma exposure (combat, suicide responses, severe accident).
- Core PTSD features: **intrusions/flashbacks**, **avoidance**, **hyperarousal**, **negative mood/cognition**, functional impairment.
- **Panic physiology** tightly linked to trauma recall (chest pain/arm tingling, palpitations, emesis).

**PTSD vs CHS on overall fit:**
- PTSD best explains the **global neuropsychiatric syndrome** (intrusions, hypervigilance, avoidance, mood/cognition).
- CHS best explains the **GI syndrome**, but does not explain the trauma-linked panic/flashbacks and long-standing postdeployment functional decline.
- Net: PTSD remains the leading *primary* diagnosis, with high likelihood of **comorbid CHS and TBI sequelae**.

**Is unexplained evidence enough to change the ranking?**
- No—because the most discriminating features (trauma-linked intrusions/hyperarousal/avoidance) strongly anchor PTSD; the GI symptoms likely represent a concurrent process (CHS and/or functional vomiting) rather than overturning the primary diagnosis.
