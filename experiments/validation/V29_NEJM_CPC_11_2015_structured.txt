Her evaluation is notable for a pronounced neutrophilic CSF pleocytosis, significant peripheral bandemia, and importantly, a history of an identical episode of bacterial meningitis at 9 years of age. 

B. INITIAL DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS:
1. **Neisseria meningitidis infection secondary to terminal complement deficiency (C5-C9):** 85% probability. The presence of acute meningitis with a classic purpuric/petechial rash is the hallmark of meningococcemia. Having a second, nearly identical episode heavily implies a specific immunodeficiency. A deficiency in the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC) impairs the ability to lyse heavily encapsulated Neisseria species, leading to recurrent, yet paradoxically less fatal, meningococcal infections.
2. **Neisseria meningitidis or Streptococcus pneumoniae infection secondary to anatomical asplenia:** 10% probability. The remote history of a motor vehicle accident hospital admission raises the possibility of a past splenectomy. However, overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI) is typically fulminant and fatal without immediate treatment, unlike the somewhat milder course and rapid stabilization seen here and presumed during the childhood episode.
3. **Pneumococcal meningitis secondary to a remote basilar skull fracture (CSF leak):** 3% probability. An old trauma could result in a dural breach, producing recurrent bacterial meningitis. However, typical CSF leaks select for S. pneumoniae, which rarely presents with this characteristic petechial/purpuric skin eruption compared to meningococcus.
4. **Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever:** 1% probability. It causes fever, headache, petechiae, and can be accompanied by mild CSF pleocytosis. However, no tick bite is noted, the CSF typically favors lymphocytes/monocytes rather than severe neutrophilia, and lifelong immunity precludes "recurrent" classical episodes.
5. **Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI) with meningitis:** 1% probability. DGI can produce a rash, though it is usually characterized by scattered pustules on an erythematous base rather than rapidly coalescing purpura. Furthermore, meningeal involvement with DGI is exceedingly rare.

C. CONSIDER ALTERNATIVES:
The single best alternative diagnosis is functional or anatomic asplenia resulting from her remote MVA. While it would explain a susceptibility to encapsulated organisms and purpura fulminans, a terminal complement deficiency provides the most accurate fit for *recurrent* N. meningitidis. Patients without a MAC paradoxically tend to survive their meningococcal sepsis episodes better than complement-replete hosts—often due to an intact reticuloendothelial system and early classical pathway opsonization clearing the bacteria, just at a slower rate. Her swift clinical stabilization on the first hospital day supports this specific defect. Properdin deficiency is another complement alternative, but it is X-linked recessive, making it exceptionally rare in a female compared to terminal complement deficiencies (such as C8 deficiency).

D. DEFEND OR UPDATE:
The recurrent nature of the disease combined with identical systemic symptoms absolutely nails the phenotype of an inherited, highly-specific immunodeficiency. N. meningitidis specifically requires the terminal complement cascade for lysis, whereas most other encapsulated bacteria can be efficiently removed by splenic capture using early complement components. Terminal complement deficiency remains the strongest, most elegant diagnostic fit.
