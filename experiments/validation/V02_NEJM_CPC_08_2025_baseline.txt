This is a complex case of a 72-year-old woman with multiple comorbidities presenting with altered mental status, respiratory failure, acute kidney injury, and hemodynamic instability requiring vasopressors. Let me systematically analyze the key findings.

**Metabolic Derangements:**
- Severe metabolic acidosis with respiratory acidosis (pH 7.16-7.19)
- Markedly elevated anion gap: 14 → 16 → 23 → **32 mmol/L** (progressively widening)
- Elevated serum osmolality: **349 mOsm/kg** (reference 280-296)
- Osmolar gap calculation: 349 - [2(143) + 230/18 + 93/2.8] = 349 - 332 = ~17 mOsm/kg (mildly elevated)
- Normal lactate throughout (0.6-1.1), ruling out lactic acidosis
- Hypercapnia (PaCO2 68-87) with inadequate respiratory compensation

**Renal:**
- Acute kidney injury: Creatinine 1.3 (baseline) → 1.81 → 3.16-3.18 mg/dL
- Hyperphosphatemia: 7.4-7.5 mg/dl
- Massive diuresis: "100-150 ml/kg/hr" (likely documentation error, but clearly polyuric)

**Clinical Context:**
- Recent high-grade urothelial carcinoma with hematuria
- Volume overload with 2.5 kg weight gain, bilateral edema, pulmonary edema
- On **metformin** and **dapagliflozin** (SGLT2 inhibitor)
- Persistent glucosuria despite AKI
- New myalgias on day 2
- HbA1c 7.5% with recent glucose readings >230 mg/dL

### Differential Diagnosis Analysis

**1. Euglycemic Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) from SGLT2 Inhibitor (Dapagliflozin)**

Supporting evidence:
- High anion gap metabolic acidosis (rising to 32)
- Ketonuria present (1+ on day 2)
- Near-normal glucose (177-230 mg/dL) - classic for euglycemic DKA
- On dapagliflozin (SGLT2 inhibitor - known cause)
- Glucosuria despite AKI (SGLT2 effect)
- Recent stressors: infection, reduced oral intake, surgery
- Normal lactate excludes lactic acidosis

**2. Metformin-Associated Lactic Acidosis (MALA)**

Against:
- **Normal lactate levels throughout (0.6-1.1 mmol/L)** - essentially rules this out
- Would expect lactate >5 mmol/L

**3. Uremic Encephalopathy**

Supporting:
- BUN 90-93 mg/dL, Creatinine 3.1-3.2 mg/dL
- Altered mental status, myalgias
- Hyperphosphatemia

Against:
- AKI alone doesn't explain the progressive high anion gap
- BUN/Cr ratio suggests prerenal component

**4. Sepsis/Urosepsis**

Supporting:
- Procalcitonin elevated (1.33), CRP >70
- Hematuria, recent bladder manipulation
- Bacteriuria, pyuria
- Requiring vasopressors

Against:
- Normal lactate argues against severe sepsis as primary driver
- Doesn't explain the markedly elevated anion gap

**5. Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure with CO2 Narcosis**

Supporting:
- PaCO2 68-87 mmHg
- Known restrictive lung disease, OSA
- Previous admission for hypercapnic somnolence
- Slept without BiPAP

Against:
- Doesn't explain the high anion gap acidosis

### Synthesis

The **progressive high anion gap acidosis (reaching 32) with normal lactate and near-normal glucose** is pathognomonic for **euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis** in a patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor. Dapagliflozin causes urinary glucose loss, which:
- Maintains near-normal serum glucose despite ketosis
- Promotes ketogenesis by lowering insulin/glucagon ratio
- Creates the "euglycemic" variant of DKA

The precipitants include:
- Acute illness (UTI/urosepsis)
- Recent procedure (cystoscopy)
- Volume depletion followed by aggressive diuresis
- Reduced oral intake

The patient also has superimposed hypercapnic respiratory failure and AKI, but the primary metabolic derangement is euglycemic DKA.
